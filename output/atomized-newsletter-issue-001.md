## INSTAGRAM — Feed Post 1 (Story-led)

I used to sit in the car park outside the gym and drive home without going in.

Not once. Regularly.

I was a police officer at the time. My mental health was in a rough place. I'd put on weight. I knew I needed to do something.

But every session felt like walking into a room where everyone knew what they were doing — except me.

So I told myself I was just not a gym person.

Here's what I've figured out since then, after working with hundreds of clients who told me the exact same thing:

The gym wasn't designed for people like me. It's designed for people who already love it.

That's not a character flaw. It's a design problem.

When I finally found the right environment and someone in my corner — everything changed. The weight came off. My mental health improved. I started looking forward to it.

That's why I became a PT.

If the car park has ever won for you — you're not lazy, you're not weak, you just haven't found your environment yet.

## INSTAGRAM — Feed Post 2 (Quick win)

The smallest shift that changed everything for my clients who hate the gym:

Stop measuring success by how hard the session was.

Measure it by whether you showed up.

That's it.

A 20-minute walk = win.
Turning up and doing half a session = win.
Going on a day you really didn't want to = massive win.

The gym industry sells intensity. "Go harder." "No excuses." "Earn it."

That's not how it works for people who are just getting started.

The habit of showing up is worth ten times more than the perfect session.

Get the habit. The results follow. Every time.

Save this for the next time you talk yourself out of going. 📌

## INSTAGRAM — Carousel (10 Slides)

**Slide 1:** "You're not a gym person." — That's what the wrong gym tells you. Here's the truth. 👇

**Slide 2:** The gym has a design problem. Most gyms are built for people who already love fitness. If you're new, anxious, or returning after years away — you're an afterthought.

**Slide 3:** That's not your fault. The equipment assumes you know how to use it. The atmosphere assumes you're confident. The culture assumes you belong. Most people don't feel like they do. Not at first.

**Slide 4:** Signs you've experienced gym anxiety: ✗ Joined but rarely gone ✗ Driven past and not gone in ✗ Felt like everyone was watching ✗ Thought "I need to get fit before I join" ✗ Stuck to the same machine every time

**Slide 5:** The biggest myth in fitness: "I'm just not a gym person." Nobody is born a gym person. Every confident regular had a first day where they had no idea what they were doing.

**Slide 6:** What actually helps — visit first. No pressure to train. Walk around. See it's just a room. Your brain stops catastrophising once it has real information.

**Slide 7:** Find the right environment. A big commercial gym is not the only option. Smaller. More personal. Somewhere someone knows your name. The environment matters as much as the programme.

**Slide 8:** Have a plan before you walk in. Even three exercises. "What am I going to do?" is half the anxiety. Know the answer before you arrive.

**Slide 9:** Measure success differently. Not: "How hard was that session?" But: "Did I show up?" The habit of going matters more than the intensity of what you do there.

"Right — what actually helps.

**Four: Go when it's quiet.** Mid-morning weekdays, early afternoon. Start then. As confidence builds, busy times stop mattering.

[B-ROLL: Quieter gym floor, early morning]

**Five: Find a familiar face.** A personal trainer solves almost every gym anxiety trigger at once. Someone who expects you, has a plan, knows your name. You're not walking into an unknown room — you're showing up to meet someone.

**Six: Reframe success.** Stop measuring success by how hard the session was. Measure by whether you showed up. A twenty-minute walk beats a session you didn't do every single time."
