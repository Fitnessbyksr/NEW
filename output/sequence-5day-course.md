Hi [First name],

Can I tell you something a bit personal?

Three years ago I was a police officer. Five years in the job altogether — started as a PCSO, then into the force proper. And by the end of it, I was in a bad place.

Not physically injured. Not anything dramatic. Just... ground down. Poor mental health, weight creeping up, sleeping badly, living on bad habits and telling myself I'd sort it out soon.

I tried the gym. Got the trainers, did the research, joined up properly.

Lasted three weeks.

And here's the thing — I wanted to go. I knew I needed to go. But every session I felt out of place, self-conscious, like everyone in there was in on something I wasn't. Like there was a manual I'd never been given.

So I stopped.

And I thought: *I'm just not a gym person.*

Sound familiar?

Here's what I've come to understand after becoming a PT and working with hundreds of people:

**Most gyms are built for people who already like the gym.**

The layout, the equipment, the atmosphere — it's all designed by people who've been training for years, for people who've been training for years. If you're new, anxious, or just not sure where to start, you're not the target market. You're an afterthought.

That's not your fault. That's a design flaw.

When I finally found the right environment — a smaller gym, a proper coach, someone who actually gave a damn — everything shifted. The weight came off. The mental health improved. I started looking forward to it.

That's what I want for you.

Tomorrow I'll show you why most fitness advice makes this problem worse — and what the industry gets completely backwards.

Cheers,

## EMAIL 4 — Day 3

**Subject:** What I'd do if I was starting from scratch tomorrow
**Alt 1:** 3 things. That's it.
**Alt 2:** Forget the gym for a second. Do this first.
**Preview text:** Genuinely, this is what I'd tell a mate who asked me where to start...

---

Hi [First name],

Right — practical stuff today.

If I was starting completely from scratch tomorrow — no fitness base, feeling a bit rough about myself, not sure where to begin — here's honestly what I'd do.

**1. I'd stop trying to overhaul everything at once.**

The people who announce they're "going clean" on Monday — cutting out sugar, going to the gym five days a week, downloading three apps — they're almost always back to square one by the following Wednesday.

Big sudden changes feel motivating for about 48 hours. Then real life arrives.

Small, boring, consistent changes are what actually work. One thing at a time.

**2. I'd move every day — but I wouldn't call it exercise.**

Walk to the shops instead of driving. Take the stairs. Ten minutes outside at lunch. Nothing that requires a gym bag.

The point is just to break the pattern of doing nothing. Your body and brain start to remember that moving feels okay.

**3. I'd find one person who's been where I am.**

Not a PT with a six-pack telling me to "commit." Someone who's actually felt rubbish, found a way through, and isn't going to judge where I'm starting from.

That's honestly the biggest one. Having the right person in your corner changes everything.

We started gently. Chair-based exercises first — I've got a qualification in that, specifically for clients who can't do traditional standing exercises. We worked with where he was, not where a fitness programme said he should be.

Slowly, session by session, things started to shift.

A few months later, that same man was doing push-ups. Squats with added weight. Getting up off the floor on his own, no problem.

I think about him a lot when I meet someone who says they're too far gone. Too old. Too unfit. Left it too late.

Nobody is too far gone.

You just need a starting point that's right for you — not one designed for someone half your age who's been training since university.
